What It Is

Product-market fit is the transition from pushing to being pulled. Before PMF, you chase customers, beg for meetings, and growth only happens when you shove it forward. After PMF, customers find you, use the product hard, refuse to give it up, and tell others.

Marc Andreessen's classic framing: "you can always feel when product-market fit isn't happening", and, just as unmistakably, when it is. It is less a metric than a state of the business you can feel in the demand.

Why It Exists

Founders waste years, and money, optimizing things that do not matter yet: scaling ad spend, hiring salespeople, polishing onboarding, raising funding, all on top of a product nobody deeply wants.

PMF is the concept that says: this is the one thing that must be true before scaling pays off.

It tells you what game you are playing: still searching for fit, or scaling a fit you have found.

How Experts Reason With It

The insight a beginner misses: PMF is a prerequisite for scaling, not a result of it. Almost every expensive startup mistake is scaling something before PMF, buying growth for a product that does not retain, so you pour customers into a leaky bucket.

  1. Retention is the truest signal. Anyone can get a spike of signups. PMF shows up as a retention curve that flattens: a stable core of users who keep coming back instead of a curve that decays to zero.
  2. The pull test beats any survey. Are users hacking around limitations to use it more? Complaining when it is down? Referring others unprompted? That desperation for the product is fit.
  3. PMF is not permanent. Markets shift, competitors arrive, your ICP evolves. You can lose fit. It is a state to maintain, not a trophy to shelve.
  4. Before PMF, do things that do not scale. Manual onboarding, hand-holding, talking to every user: this is how you find fit, and it is the opposite of the efficiency instinct an engineer brings.

Founder takeaway: before PMF, your job is learning, not growing. Spending on CAC or hiring before PMF converts runway into noise.

When You Use It

  • As the gate before scaling anything: spend, hiring, fundraising.
  • To diagnose "we launched but it is quiet": usually a fit problem, not a marketing one.
  • To decide whether to persevere or pivot: no fit after honest effort means changing the problem, ICP, or product rather than pushing harder on the same thing.

Metrics and Measurement

There is no single number, but these proxies make PMF concrete enough to track.

Sean Ellis Test

Ask users how they would feel if they could no longer use the product. At or above 40% "very disappointed" is a widely cited signal.

Retention Curves

Look for cohort usage that flattens at a healthy level instead of decaying toward zero.

Organic Pull

Unprompted referrals, word-of-mouth growth, inbound demand, and users pulling the product into more workflows.

Usage Intensity

Frequency and depth of use relative to the job the product does for its target customer.

Common Misconceptions

  • "Launch = PMF." Launching is an event; PMF is sustained pull. Silence after launch means you do not have it yet.
  • "More features -> PMF." Usually the opposite. Fit comes from solving one problem deeply for a specific ICP, not from breadth.
  • "Once you have it, you are safe." Fit can erode; it must be maintained.
  • "It is a vibe, not measurable." It is partly felt, but retention curves and the Sean Ellis test make it concrete enough to track.

Examples

  • Superhuman famously engineered its way to PMF by driving the Sean Ellis "very disappointed" score above 40% through a tight feedback loop with its most engaged users.
  • Slack began as an internal tool that users pulled into every team unprompted; the pull was obvious long before any growth spend.

Talking To An LLM About It

Weak prompt

"How do I know if my product is good?"

Better prompt

"How do I know if my SaaS has product-market fit?"

Expert prompt

"For a bootstrapped B2B SaaS targeting recruiting agencies, help me design a lightweight PMF measurement plan: which retention/cohort metric to track, how to run the Sean Ellis 40% test with my first ~30 users, what 'good' looks like, and clear signals that would tell me to pivot rather than keep pushing."

The expert version names the ICP and asks for measurement and decision criteria, so the model gives an operating plan instead of a definition.

My Evolving Understanding

v1 (2026-07-01): First pass. The reframe that landed: I thought of PMF as "people like the product." The sharper model is pull vs. push. Before PMF you are shoving, after PMF the market pulls, and the truest evidence is a flattening retention curve, not signups or praise.

Biggest behavioral implication: PMF is a gate, and the engineer's instinct to optimize, scale, and automate is exactly the wrong instinct to indulge before you are through it.
