چیست؟

A pipeline is a visual board of every deal currently in flight, sorted into stages that run left to right:

| Stage | The seller's lazy label (an activity) | What the stage really is (a buyer commitment) | Exit criterion (what must be true to advance) | |---|---|---|---| | Lead | "I added them to the CRM" | a name that fits the ICP — no commitment yet | matches ICP; contactable | | MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead) | "they downloaded a whitepaper" | the buyer raised a hand — showed intent, not just eyeballs | took an intent action (demo request, pricing page, repeated visits) | | SQL (Sales Qualified Lead) | "I sent them an email" | the buyer agreed to a real conversation and has a problem worth solving | discovery call held; a genuine pain confirmed | | Opportunity | "I ran a demo" | the buyer confirmed there is a deal to pursue — budget, authority, need, timeline all real | qualified (BANT / MEDDIC); a mutually-agreed next step on the calendar | | Closed-won / Closed-lost | — | the buyer signed, or explicitly walked | contract signed or a clear "no" (never a limbo "maybe") |

The sales cycle is the time one deal spends crossing that whole board — days for a $20/mo self-serve tool, 6–18 months for a six-figure enterprise contract.

The single most important idea, stated up front: a stage is a state of the buyer's mind, not a task on the seller's to-do list. "I gave a demo" is something you did; it tells you nothing about whether the buyer moved. The deal only advances when the buyer commits to the next step.

چرا وجود دارد؟

Left to instinct, selling is a black box: some deals close, some vanish, and the founder has no idea why or when the next dollar arrives. Revenue feels like weather — something that happens to you.

The pipeline exists to make that black box legible and forecastable. If you know that historically 100 leads yield 25 SQLs yield 8 opportunities yield 2 closed deals, then revenue stops being luck: to close 10 deals next quarter you need ~50 opportunities, which means ~1,250 leads in the top — a number you can actually go manufacture. Aaron Ross's Predictable Revenue is named for exactly this promise: turn sales from a hope into a machine with known inputs and outputs. The pipeline is the instrument that lets you run the machine backward from a revenue target to the activity required today.

استدلال متخصص‌ها

The insight a beginner misses: stages are buyer commitments, and the only honest pipeline is one where every stage change is triggered by the buyer, not the seller.

  1. Every stage is a buyer commitment. The reframe that changes everything: rewrite

each stage from "what I did" to "what the buyer did." Not "I demoed" but "the buyer agreed the demo solved a real pain and looped in their boss." Activity-based stages are the original sin of pipeline management — they let a rep feel progress (busy! emails sent! demos given!) while no buyer has actually moved an inch. A stage with no buyer commitment behind it is a lie you tell your own forecast.

  1. Exit criteria per stage, or the pipeline rots. Each stage needs a hard,

objective gate a deal must clear to advance — "budget confirmed," "champion identified," "next meeting booked." Without exit criteria, reps advance deals on optimism, the pipeline inflates, and the forecast becomes fiction. This is where qualification frameworks earn their keep: BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) is the simple version; MEDDIC (Metrics, Economic buyer, Decision criteria, Decision process, Identify pain, Champion) is the rigorous one built for complex B2B deals — it forces you to prove you've reached the person who controls the money and mapped how they actually decide.

  1. Cycle length must match the GTM motion and the price. You do not get

to choose your cycle length — the price point and motion impose it. A $30/mo product cannot carry a six-month, human-heavy cycle: the CAC would dwarf the revenue. Low price → self-serve / product-led, cycle measured in minutes. Mid price → sales-assisted, cycle in weeks. High price (five/six figures) → enterprise, cycle in quarters, with committees and procurement. Mismatch here is fatal: a self-serve price with an enterprise cycle bleeds money on every deal.

| Motion | Price band | Typical cycle | Who does the "selling" | |---|---|---|---| | Self-serve / PLG | < ~$50/mo | minutes–days | the product (aarrr-funnel is the pipeline) | | Sales-assisted | ~$100s–low $1,000s/mo | weeks | a rep + the product together | | Enterprise | $10k–$1M+/yr | months–quarters | a rep, an SE, execs, procurement |

  1. Pipeline coverage: carry 3–4× your target. Because deals leak at every stage, you

must hold 3–4× your revenue target in open pipeline to hit it. Want $100k this quarter and deals close at ~30%? You need ~$300k+ of qualified opportunity today. "Coverage" is the early-warning system: thin coverage this month = a miss you can see coming two months out and still fix.

  1. *Conversion math is CAC.* The pipeline is a funnel of conversion

rates, and those rates set your acquisition cost. If it takes 1,250 leads and one rep's quarter to produce 10 customers, the fully-loaded cost of those leads + that salary is your CAC. Improving a mid-funnel conversion rate (better qualification, tighter ICP) lowers CAC without spending a cent more on top-of-funnel — the same leverage the AARRR funnel teaches: fix the leak, don't just pour in more.

  1. *Specialize the roles (the Predictable Revenue move).* Ross's core lesson at

Salesforce: stop asking one person to prospect, close, and farm. Split the pipeline into specialized roles — SDRs (prospect and qualify: lead → SQL), AEs (close: opportunity → won), CSMs (retain and expand post-sale). Each stage of the pipeline gets an owner who is expert at that transition. This is the sales-org version of "one owner per funnel stage."

The founder's takeaway: the pipeline is a forecasting instrument, and it only tells the truth if stages are buyer commitments with hard exit criteria. Everything else — coverage, conversion, CAC, role design — is downstream of that one discipline.

زمان استفاده

  • Forecasting revenue — running a target backward through your conversion rates to

find how much pipeline you must build today.

  • Diagnosing a stall — locating the stage with the steepest drop-off (e.g. lots of

demos, few opportunities → your qualification or your ICP is off).

  • Sizing the cycle to the price — sanity-checking that your GTM motion

and pricing can actually afford the cycle the deal demands.

  • Deciding when to hire sales — a repeatable pipeline with known conversion is the

prerequisite for handing selling to someone who isn't you.

  • Cleaning the CRM — killing zombie deals that have no buyer commitment behind them,

so the forecast reflects reality.

سنجه‌ها و اندازه‌گیری

  • Sales cycle length: average days from first touch (or SQL) to closed-won. The

clock you're trying to shorten.

  • Stage conversion rates: the % that advance from each stage to the next; the

product of them all = overall lead→customer conversion (the same multiply-don't-add math as aarrr-funnel).

  • Win rate: closed-won ÷ (closed-won + closed-lost). Rising win rate often means

better qualification upstream, not better closing.

  • Pipeline coverage ratio: open qualified pipeline ÷ revenue target. Aim ~3–4×.
  • Average deal size / ACV (Annual Contract Value): sets how many deals you need and,

with cycle length, how much runway a deal consumes.

  • Pipeline velocity: (# open opps × win rate × avg deal size) ÷ cycle length — one

number for "how fast is money moving through the machine." Pull any lever (more opps, higher win rate, bigger deals, shorter cycle) and velocity rises.

برداشت‌های غلط رایج

  • "If the product is good, it sells itself." The engineer's blind spot, and the most

expensive one. A great product lowers the cost of selling; it does not remove it. Buyers must find you, trust you, and decide — that's distribution and a pipeline, not magic. Products don't sell themselves; they get sold (or distributed) or they die unknown.

  • "Stages are what the salesperson does." No — this is the activity-based-stage trap.

Stages are buyer commitments. "I sent a proposal" is not a stage; "the buyer's economic buyer agreed to review the proposal by Friday" is.

  • "A fuller pipeline is a healthier pipeline." Not if it's stuffed with deals that

fail their exit criteria. Inflated pipeline is worse than thin pipeline — it lies to your forecast. This is sandbagging in reverse; both distort the truth.

  • "Sandbagging is harmless." Sandbagging — a rep hiding deals or lowballing the

forecast to look like a hero later — corrupts the one thing the pipeline exists to give you: an honest number. So does its opposite, "happy ears" over-optimism. The pipeline is only worth having if it's calibrated.

  • "Longer cycle = bigger deal = better." A cycle longer than your pricing

and CAC can support is a slow bleed. Match the cycle to the economics.

  • "We just need more leads." Usually the leak is mid-funnel (qualification), not the

top. More leads into a leaky pipeline just raises CAC.

مثال‌ها

  • Working the machine backward. A B2B SaaS wants $120k in new ARR next quarter at

a $12k ACV → needs 10 new customers. History: 30% of opportunities close, 25% of SQLs become opportunities, 20% of leads become SQLs. So 10 wins ⇒ ~33 opportunities ⇒ ~132 SQLs ⇒ ~660 leads. That 660 is now a manufacturable target, and at ~3× coverage the team should be carrying ~$360k of open pipeline today to trust the $120k. Revenue stops being weather.

  • Price/cycle mismatch that kills. A team prices a product at $40/mo but runs a

consultative, multi-call, three-month enterprise cycle on each deal. Even at a perfect close rate, one rep might land a handful of $40/mo accounts per quarter — CAC dwarfs revenue by orders of magnitude. The fix isn't "sell harder"; it's re-architecting to self-serve so the product runs the cycle (see gtm, aarrr-funnel).

  • Exit criteria save a forecast. A pipeline shows $500k "committed." Applying MEDDIC

reveals half the deals have no identified economic buyer and no mapped decision process — they fail the Opportunity exit criteria. Honest pipeline: ~$250k. Painful, but now the forecast is real and the reps know exactly what to go get (reach the money, map the decision).

حرف زدن با LLM درباره آن

  • Weak prompt: "How do I sell my software?"
  • Better prompt: "How do I build a sales pipeline for my B2B SaaS and define the

stages?"

  • Expert prompt: "For a sales-assisted B2B SaaS at a $12k ACV targeting recruiting

agencies, design a pipeline where each stage is a buyer commitment with explicit exit criteria (use MEDDIC for the opportunity stage). Given a $120k new-ARR quarterly target and historical conversions of 20% lead→SQL, 25% SQL→opp, 30% opp→won, work backward to the leads and pipeline coverage I need, estimate the implied CAC, and flag whether my ~3-week cycle is consistent with this price and GTM motion."

The expert version supplies the motion, price, ICP, a revenue target, and real conversion rates — so the model does pipeline engineering (stage design, coverage math, CAC implication) instead of handing back a generic "do outbound and follow up" list. The magic word is "buyer commitment with exit criteria": it pulls the model straight into the expert mental model instead of the activity-based-stage trap.

فهم در حال تکامل من

  • v1 (2026-07-04): First pass. The reframe that landed hardest: I instinctively read

pipeline stages as my to-do list — lead, email, demo, proposal, close, all things I do. The expert mental model flips it entirely: a stage is a commitment the buyer made, and a deal only advances when the buyer moves, gated by objective exit criteria. That single shift is what turns a pipeline from theater into a forecasting instrument. Second landing: cycle length isn't a choice — the price and GTM motion impose it, and mismatching them (self-serve price, enterprise cycle) quietly destroys unit economics via CAC. And the engineer's comforting lie — "a good product sells itself" — is exactly backwards: products get sold and distributed, or they die unknown (distribution).

منابع

  • *Aaron Ross & Marylou Tyler, Predictable Revenue (2011)* — the "$100M" Salesforce

playbook: specialize sales roles (SDR / AE / CSM), separate prospecting from closing, and treat pipeline as a machine with known inputs → outputs. The origin of the "predictable" framing used throughout this note.

  • MEDDIC (Metrics, Economic buyer, Decision criteria, Decision process, Identify

pain, Champion) — a B2B qualification framework developed at PTC in the 1990s (Dick Dunkel & Jack Napoli); used here as the rigorous set of exit criteria for the Opportunity stage. BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline), originated by IBM, is the lighter-weight version.

  • Consultative / solution selling (Mack Hanan, Consultative Selling; Rackham,

SPIN Selling) — the lineage behind "sell to the buyer's confirmed pain," reinforcing the buyer-commitment reframe (to verify and deepen on revisit).

  • See also gtm, cac, icp, aarrr-funnel,

distribution.
